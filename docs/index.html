Predicting NCAA Tournament Success from Team Efficiency Metrics

Built a model to predict NCAA Tournament results using advanced team stats like offense, defense, shooting, turnovers, and pace. The model predicts if a team makes the tournament, their seed range, and how far they go. Includes clear charts and feature rankings.

Python scikit-learn pandas matplotlib Jupyter Notebooks numpy seaborn
More details

Problem

It is hard to predict NCAA Tournament results. Advanced stats give a better view of team strength, but it is not clear which ones matter most. This project finds which stats best predict who makes the tournament, their seed, and how they perform.

Approach

Collected and cleaned data from multiple seasons. Focused on team stats like offense, defense, shooting, turnovers, and pace. Explored the data to find patterns. Trained models like logistic regression, random forest, and XGBoost. I then ested them with cross-validation. This helped me so I could measure which stats had the most impact.

Results & Impact

The results showed that team resume mattered most. Wins Above Bubble, BARTHAG, and total wins were the strongest predictors in every model. Offensive and defensive efficiency also helped explain success. Logistic Regression was easy to read but less accurate, while Random Forest and the Neural Network gave better overall results by handling more complex patterns. The data had more non-tournament teams, which hurt performance, especially for Logistic Regression. Overall, stronger resumes and solid efficiency stats led to the best predictions.

Spartan Football Crowd Cheer Analysis

Analyzed audio and attendance data from Spartan Stadium to understand what game events drive the loudest crowd reactions. Results were used to help the athletics department optimize in-game entertainment and — personally — to determine the exact moments when I should run onto the field for maximum dramatic effect.

Python pandas seaborn Plotly Jupyter Notebooks
More details

Problem

Fan energy is contagious, but it is also unpredictable. The athletics department wanted to know which in-game events (touchdowns, turnovers, big defensive stops, walk-up music choices) produced the biggest crowd reactions — and whether the pattern differed by opponent, weather, or game time. As the mascot, I had strong professional interest in this question.

Approach

Combined play-by-play game data with decibel readings from stadium microphones across three seasons (~36 home games). Engineered features for score differential, down-and-distance, quarter, temperature, and opponent rank. Produced interactive Plotly dashboards that let non-technical staff filter by season and event type to explore crowd response patterns on their own.

Results & Impact

Defensive turnovers produced the loudest average crowd response — louder even than touchdowns — especially when MSU was trailing. Walk-up music selections in the 3rd quarter showed a statistically significant effect on cheer volume for games decided by fewer than 10 points. The athletics team used these findings to redesign the third-quarter entertainment package for the following season. I was given extra treats for my contributions.
